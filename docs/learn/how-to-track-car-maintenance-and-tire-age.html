How to track car maintenance and tire age

Why Keeping Records Actually Matters

Most people know roughly when they last changed their oil, but ask them about their rear differential fluid or the manufacture date stamped on their tire sidewall and the conversation gets quiet fast. The stakes are real: tires degrade from the inside out even when they look fine, and skipped maintenance intervals compound into expensive failures. A good tracking system turns guesswork into a schedule you can trust.

Start With What You Already Have

Before building any system, gather your existing records.

Check the glove box and service history folder. Dealerships and quick-lube shops often hand back a paper receipt. Collect every one you can find.

Pull your car's VIN history. Services like Carfax or the NHTSA VIN lookup tool can show major service events, especially if the car has changed hands. This is particularly useful for used vehicles where you're inheriting a maintenance gap.

Look for stickers. Oil change shops typically put a small sticker on your windshield or doorjamb showing the mileage and date of the last service. Tire shops sometimes write the install date on the tire itself with a grease pencil.

Check your bank or credit card statements. Search for auto parts stores, dealerships, and service stations going back as far as you can. This reconstructs a rough timeline even when paper receipts are gone.

Once you have that baseline, write it all down in one place — a simple spreadsheet works perfectly well.

Building Your Maintenance Tracking Spreadsheet

A spreadsheet with these columns covers almost everything:

| Date | Mileage | Service Type | Cost | Next Due (miles) | Next Due (date) | Notes | |---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

The "next due" columns are the most important. Every car maintenance task has two triggers — a mileage interval and a time interval — and the first one reached wins. For example, engine oil might be rated for 7,500 miles or 12 months, whichever comes first. If you drive very little, the time trigger will fire before the mileage one.

What to include in your service list

Group services by how frequently they occur:

Every few months or a few thousand miles:

  • Engine oil and filter
  • Tire pressure check (technically ongoing, but log it)
  • Washer fluid

Every 15,000–30,000 miles or 1–2 years:

  • Air filter (engine)
  • Cabin air filter
  • Tire rotation
  • Brake inspection
  • Battery terminal cleaning

Every 30,000–60,000 miles or 2–5 years:

  • Brake fluid flush
  • Coolant flush
  • Spark plugs (varies widely by vehicle)
  • Transmission fluid
  • Power steering fluid

Every 60,000–100,000+ miles:

  • Timing belt (if equipped — not all cars have one)
  • Serpentine belt
  • Differential fluids
  • Transfer case fluid (4WD/AWD vehicles)

Your owner's manual is the authoritative source for your specific vehicle. Don't rely on the generic intervals posted at oil change shops — they're conservative estimates designed for the average car, not yours.

How to Track Tire Age Specifically

Tires are where people make the most dangerous assumptions. A tire that looks perfectly fine on the outside can be dangerously degraded internally due to oxidation and UV exposure.

Reading the DOT code

Every tire sold in the United States has a DOT (Department of Transportation) code molded into the sidewall. The last four digits are what you need. They represent the week and year of manufacture.

For example: DOT XXXXXXXX 2319

That means the tire was manufactured in the 23rd week of 2019.

To find the code, look on the inner or outer sidewall for the letters "DOT" followed by a string of letters and numbers. The four-digit date code is always at the very end.

When to replace tires based on age

The general industry consensus — used by most vehicle manufacturers and tire companies — is:

  • Replace at 6 years regardless of tread depth if the tires show any cracking, hardening, or sidewall damage
  • Replace at 10 years maximum regardless of appearance, even if tread looks fine

Some manufacturers set their own limits. BMW, for instance, recommends replacement at 6 years from the manufacture date. Check your owner's manual for your vehicle's specific guidance.

A tire manufactured in week 2319 (May 2019) should be seriously evaluated by 2025 and replaced no later than 2029 — but ideally sooner if the vehicle is used in hot climates, which accelerates rubber degradation.

Log your tires individually

When you install tires, record all four DOT codes in your spreadsheet. Tires from the same batch won't always be from the same production week, and spare tires are frequently forgotten until they're needed in an emergency.

Add a column for each tire position:

| Position | Brand/Model | DOT Code | Install Date | Tread Depth (new) | Tread Depth (current) | Replace By |

Update tread depth every time you rotate. Use a tread depth gauge — they cost a few dollars — and measure in the center groove and both outer grooves. When any measurement hits 2/32 of an inch, the tire is legally worn out in most states. At 4/32, braking distance in wet conditions starts to increase noticeably.

Setting Up Your Reminder System

A log that no one checks isn't useful. Build the reminders into whatever you already look at.

Calendar reminders: Set a recurring annual reminder to review your maintenance log. Also set specific future reminders for time-sensitive items — brake fluid flush in March 2026, tire age evaluation in June 2025, and so on.

Mileage-based reminders: Each time you log a service, calculate the target mileage for the next one and write it on a sticky note on your dashboard until you hit it, or note it in your spreadsheet and check it monthly when you fill up with gas.

Photograph everything: Take a photo of your odometer alongside every receipt. Store the photos in a dated folder on your phone. This creates a backup record that's searchable and timestamped.

Keep a physical binder: Especially if multiple people drive the car. A physical folder in the glove box with the latest receipts and a one-page summary sheet is harder to lose than a phone.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Trusting "looks fine" as an inspection standard. Rubber degradation, brake pad wear, belt cracking, and coolant contamination are not visible to casual inspection. Use intervals, not eyeballs.

Tracking by mileage only. If you drive 8,000 miles a year, your brake fluid still needs flushing every two years whether you've hit the mileage threshold or not. Time-based intervals matter independently of mileage.

Forgetting the spare. The spare tire often has a manufacture date years before you bought the car. Check its DOT code now. A 12-year-old spare that you've never thought about is not a safety net.

Not transferring records when you sell. If you sell the car with a complete maintenance history, you can ask more for it — and you're genuinely helping the next owner's safety. Keep a copy for yourself; it's useful for warranty or insurance disputes.

Assuming the previous owner did everything. On a used car, assume nothing has been done until you can verify it. Replace brake fluid, cabin air filter, and engine air filter on purchase. Budget for the rest based on mileage milestones.

Keeping It Sustainable

The system only works if you actually use it. Keep it simple enough that you'll open it when you get home from an oil change rather than thinking "I'll do it later."

One approach that works well: keep a spreadsheet pinned in your browser bookmarks. After any service — including ones you do yourself — open it immediately and fill in the row while the receipt is in your hand. Set the next-due reminders the same day. That five-minute habit, done consistently, gives you a complete vehicle history that makes every future service decision easier and every used-car sale more valuable.

FAQ

How often should I actually check tire pressure? Once a month and before any long trip. Tire pressure drops roughly 1 PSI for every 10°F drop in ambient temperature, so check more frequently in fall and winter. Always check when the tires are cold (driven less than a mile).

What's the difference between tire age and tread life? Tread life is about how much rubber is left on the contact patch. Tire age is about how long the rubber compound has been degrading, which happens regardless of use. You can have a tire with plenty of tread depth that is still dangerously old.

Do I need to track the spare tire separately? Yes. Spare tires are often forgotten, but they have the same age-based degradation as regular tires and are frequently much older than the tires on the vehicle.

Is it worth tracking maintenance for a high-mileage car I plan to sell soon? Yes. A documented service history, even partial, is worth real money in a private sale and can help you negotiate trade-in value at a dealership.

What if I don't have the owner's manual? Most manufacturers post digital copies on their websites. Search your year, make, and model plus "owner's manual PDF." NHTSA's website also maintains a vehicle lookup tool with some service information.
