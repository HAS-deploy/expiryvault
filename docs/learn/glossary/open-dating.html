open dating — what it means for shelf life

What it means

Open dating refers to a calendar date printed on food packaging in plain, readable language — a specific month, day, and/or year that consumers can actually understand. Unlike coded dating systems used by manufacturers for internal tracking, open dating is designed to be read and interpreted by the general public. Common forms include phrases like "Best By June 15," "Use By 03/22/2026," or "Sell By Aug 10."

Open dating is voluntary in most countries for shelf-stable products, though it is required by law for certain categories like infant formula in the United States. The date can signal peak quality, a retailer's stocking deadline, or a safety cutoff, depending on the label phrase used.

Why it matters

Open dating shows up every time you check whether food in your fridge or pantry is still good. It gives consumers a straightforward reference point for making decisions about freshness and food safety without needing to decode cryptographic lot numbers or manufacturing codes. Retailers use open dates to manage inventory rotation, pulling or discounting products as dates approach. For regulators and food banks, open dating is central to donation policies and food waste reduction efforts, since misunderstanding these dates leads to millions of pounds of still-edible food being thrown away each year.

Example

A carton of eggs printed with "Best By Sep 4" is using open dating. Anyone picking up the carton can immediately see the date without consulting a guide or contacting the manufacturer. Compare this to a can of soup stamped with "L3247B092" — that's a closed or coded date only the producer can decipher. The egg carton is open-dated; the soup can is not.

Common confusions

  • Open dating vs. closed/coded dating: These are opposites. Open dating is consumer-readable; closed dating uses manufacturer codes the public isn't meant to decode directly.
  • Open dating vs. a specific date type: "Open dating" is the umbrella term for any human-readable date on packaging. It doesn't tell you what kind of date it is — you still need to check whether it says "Best By," "Use By," "Sell By," or "Freeze By," because each phrase means something different.
  • Assuming all open dates are safety dates: Most open dates indicate quality, not safety. A "Best By" date means the product may taste or perform best before that date, but it isn't necessarily unsafe afterward. Only "Use By" dates on certain products (like deli meats) are typically treated as hard safety cutoffs.
