expiration date — what it means for shelf life

What it means

An expiration date is a manufacturer-printed date indicating when a product is no longer considered safe or effective to use or consume. After this date, the product may have degraded in quality, potency, or safety to a point where using it carries real risk. It is a hard cutoff, distinct from softer freshness indicators, and is most commonly found on medications, infant formula, certain medical devices, and some perishable foods.

For regulated products like prescription drugs and over-the-counter medications, expiration dates are legally required and backed by stability testing. The manufacturer tests the product under controlled conditions and guarantees that it meets its labeled specifications — in terms of potency, purity, and safety — up until that printed date.

Why it matters

Expiration dates come up whenever you need to trust that a product will perform as intended. For medications, using an expired product might mean getting a lower-than-expected dose of a critical drug. For infant formula, the nutritional content may degrade, posing real health risks to a vulnerable population. In food, expiration dates (when they appear) signal microbial safety thresholds, not just quality preferences. Pharmacists, food safety regulators, and healthcare providers treat expiration dates as the definitive line between a product being fit for use and not.

Example

A bottle of amoxicillin capsules has an expiration date of 09/2024. Using those capsules in January 2025 means the antibiotic may have lost enough potency that it fails to adequately treat an infection — potentially allowing bacteria to persist or develop resistance. A pharmacist would advise discarding them and getting a fresh prescription.

Common confusions

  • Best-by date vs. expiration date: A best-by date is about peak quality — flavor, texture, freshness. An expiration date signals a safety or efficacy threshold. Many people use these interchangeably, but they are not the same. Eating a chip past its best-by date is harmless; taking an expired EpiPen in an emergency may not be.
  • Use-by date: Very similar to expiration date and often used interchangeably for perishable foods, but "use-by" is more common in food labeling while "expiration date" is more common in pharmaceuticals and medical contexts.
  • Sell-by date: This is a retailer-facing date about inventory management, not a direct signal to the consumer about safety. A product sold on its sell-by date still has usable life remaining.
  • Thinking expired always means dangerous: For many non-critical products — canned goods, some dry staples — quality degrades gradually after the date but the product isn't immediately hazardous. For medications, formula, and sterile products, the calculus is stricter.
