medication beyond-use date — what it means for shelf life

What it means

A beyond-use date (BUD) is the date after which a medication should no longer be used. Unlike a manufacturer's expiration date, a BUD is assigned by a pharmacist after a drug has been compounded, repackaged, diluted, or reconstituted. It reflects how long the medication is expected to remain stable and safe under the specific storage conditions and in the container it has been placed in — not the original sealed packaging from the factory.

BUDs are governed by standards set by organizations like the United States Pharmacopeia (USP), particularly chapters USP <795> (non-sterile) and USP <797> (sterile preparations). These standards define maximum allowable timeframes based on factors like whether the drug is sterile, how it was prepared, and how it will be stored (room temperature, refrigerated, or frozen).

Why it matters

Beyond-use dates come up whenever a medication leaves its original, sealed manufacturer container. Common situations include:

  • A pharmacist mixes a liquid antibiotic suspension from powder and water — the BUD is typically 10–14 days, even if the powder's expiration date is a year away.
  • A hospital pharmacy prepares IV bags or syringes in advance; each gets a BUD based on USP <797> guidelines.
  • A compounding pharmacy makes a custom cream or capsule formulation; the BUD is assigned based on the ingredients, preservatives, and storage conditions.
  • A patient transfers pills into a weekly pill organizer — technically, the original expiration date no longer formally applies in the same way once medications are removed from the labeled container.

Example

A pharmacist receives a vial of amoxicillin powder with a manufacturer expiration date of December 2026. After adding water to reconstitute it into a liquid suspension, the pharmacist labels the bottle with a beyond-use date of 14 days from today. Even though the powder would have been "good" until 2026, the liquid form degrades much faster and is only reliable for two weeks — the BUD reflects that new reality.

Common confusions

BUD vs. expiration date: The expiration date is set by the manufacturer based on stability testing of the product in its original, unopened container. A BUD is assigned after the product has been altered or repackaged, and it is almost always shorter than the remaining shelf life suggested by the expiration date.

BUD vs. "use by" date on food: These phrases sound similar but food "use by" dates are primarily about safety from spoilage, while medication BUDs are about chemical stability and potency — a drug past its BUD may not be obviously spoiled but could be less effective or potentially harmful.

Assuming the expiration date still applies: Once you reconstitute, dilute, or repackage a medication, the original expiration date is no longer the controlling date. The BUD takes over, and it should be followed strictly.
