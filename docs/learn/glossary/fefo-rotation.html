FEFO rotation — what it means for shelf life

What it means

FEFO stands for First Expired, First Out. It is an inventory rotation method where items with the earliest expiration (or best-before) dates are picked, sold, or used before items with later expiration dates — regardless of when the stock was received. The goal is to ensure no product expires on the shelf or in the warehouse simply because newer stock was pulled first.

FEFO is a refinement of the more general FIFO (First In, First Out) rule. While FIFO assumes that older stock also has an earlier expiration date, FEFO ignores receipt date entirely and focuses only on the expiration date stamped on the product.

Why it matters

FEFO is critical in any industry where products have fixed expiration dates and the consequences of selling or using expired goods are serious. This includes food and beverage distribution, pharmaceuticals, medical devices, cosmetics, and chemical storage. A warehouse receiving multiple batches of the same product — perhaps from different suppliers or with different manufacturing dates — may find that a newer delivery actually expires sooner than older stock. FEFO catches this mismatch that FIFO would miss.

Retailers and distributors who fail to enforce FEFO risk product write-offs, customer complaints, regulatory violations, and in the worst cases, public health incidents. Many food safety standards, including those under HACCP frameworks, and pharmaceutical regulations explicitly require FEFO-compliant rotation.

Example

A grocery distributor receives two pallets of yogurt on the same day. Pallet A has a use-by date of June 10; Pallet B has a use-by date of June 4 — perhaps because it came from a different production run. Under strict FIFO, Pallet A (received first, or stacked in front) might be shipped out first. Under FEFO, Pallet B must go out first because it expires sooner. Without FEFO, a retailer could receive Pallet A and have only a few days of shelf life left once Pallet B finally ships.

Common confusions

  • FEFO vs. FIFO: FIFO rotates by receipt date; FEFO rotates by expiration date. In practice these often align, but whenever batch dates vary, only FEFO guarantees the shortest-lived product ships first.
  • FEFO vs. LIFO: LIFO (Last In, First Out) is used for cost accounting in some industries but is the opposite of good perishable rotation and is generally unsuitable for products with expiration dates.
  • Best-before vs. use-by: FEFO applies to whichever date is printed on the product — best-before, use-by, or expiry date — but the type of date matters for determining whether a product is legally sellable. FEFO tells you the order to move stock; the date type tells you whether it's still permitted for sale at all.
