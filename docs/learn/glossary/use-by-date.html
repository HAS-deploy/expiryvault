use by date — what it means for shelf life

What it means

A use by date is a safety deadline printed on perishable food products indicating the last day the item is considered safe to eat, provided it has been stored correctly. After this date, the food may pose a health risk even if it looks, smells, and tastes completely normal — harmful bacteria can multiply to dangerous levels without any obvious signs of spoilage.

Use by dates are set by manufacturers based on food safety testing, not just quality assessments. They are legally regulated in many countries, meaning producers must be confident the food is safe up to that date under recommended storage conditions (typically refrigeration at a specific temperature).

Why it matters

This date matters most for high-risk, perishable products: raw meat, fish, ready-to-eat meals, soft cheeses, and pre-packaged deli items. These foods support rapid bacterial growth — including pathogens like Listeria, Salmonella, and E. coli — that can cause serious illness. Unlike quality-based dates, the use by date is the one you should treat as a hard limit. Eating food past its use by date is generally not advisable, even if the food seems fine.

Retailers and food service businesses often have legal obligations not to sell products past their use by date, making this date critical in commercial settings as well.

Example

A package of raw chicken purchased on Monday has a use by date of Wednesday. Even if it has been kept refrigerated and smells normal on Thursday, it should be discarded — not cooked to "kill the bacteria," because some toxins produced by bacteria are heat-stable and won't be neutralized by cooking.

Common confusions

  • Best before date: This is the most common mix-up. A best before date is about quality, not safety. Foods past their best before date may taste stale or lose nutritional value, but they are often still safe to eat. Use by is about safety; best before is about peak quality.
  • Sell by / display until date: These are stock management instructions for retailers, not consumer safety guides. They indicate when a shop should pull a product from shelves, usually to allow the consumer time to use it at home before the actual use by date.
  • Assuming cooking fixes it: Cooking does kill many bacteria, but it doesn't neutralize all toxins that form in food that has already spoiled or become unsafe. The use by date remains relevant even when food will be thoroughly cooked.
