closed dating — what it means for shelf life

What it means

Closed dating is a system manufacturers use to stamp products with coded information about production or expiration dates that the average consumer cannot easily read without knowing the code. Unlike open dating — which uses plain language like "Best By June 2026" — closed dating uses letter-number combinations, Julian date codes, or other proprietary formats. The codes are primarily intended for inventory tracking, distribution management, and quality control purposes within the supply chain.

Why it matters

Closed dating comes up most often when you find a product with no human-readable date and you're trying to figure out whether it's still good. It's common on shelf-stable canned goods, dry groceries, beverages, and some household products. Retailers and distributors use these codes to rotate stock and trace products during recalls. Consumers occasionally encounter them when a "best by" date has worn off or was never printed in plain text. In recall situations, the FDA or USDA may ask you to check a closed date code to determine whether your specific lot is affected — making the ability to decode it practically important.

Example

A can of soup is stamped with A0322B14. This might decode as: A = January (A being the first letter of the alphabet), 03 = day 3, 22 = year 2022, B = the manufacturing plant code, 14 = the production shift or batch. Without the manufacturer's key, you'd have no idea what this means. Calling the brand's customer service line or checking their website often reveals the decoding method.

Common confusions

Closed dating vs. open dating: Open dating is the readable calendar date printed for consumers ("Use By," "Sell By," "Best By"). Closed dating is encoded and not immediately consumer-friendly. Many products carry both.

Closed dating vs. Julian dates: Julian date codes (where the day of the year is expressed as a number from 001–365) are one type of closed dating, but not all closed dating uses Julian format. The terms aren't interchangeable.

Closed dating as a safety indicator: Closed dates are usually production or packaging dates, not safety cutoff dates. Seeing a production date doesn't tell you when the product becomes unsafe — only the manufacturer's guidelines or a separate best-by date can tell you that.
