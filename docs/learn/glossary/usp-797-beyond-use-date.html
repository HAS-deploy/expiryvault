USP <797> beyond-use date — what it means for shelf life

What it means

USP <797> beyond-use date (BUD) is the date and time after which a compounded sterile preparation (CSP) — such as an IV bag, injectable solution, or eye drop made in a pharmacy — must not be used. It is assigned by the compounder based on United States Pharmacopeia Chapter <797> guidelines, which set maximum allowable timeframes depending on the preparation's sterility risk category, storage conditions, and whether sterility testing was performed. Unlike a manufacturer's expiration date, the BUD is determined at the time of compounding and is always equal to or shorter than any ingredient's expiration date.

The guidelines specify default BUDs organized by category: preparations can fall under Category 1 (shorter BUDs, no sterility testing required) or Category 2 (longer BUDs permitted when full sterility and endotoxin testing is conducted). Storage temperature — room temperature, refrigerated, or frozen — also directly affects the allowable BUD, with refrigeration and freezing generally extending the permissible window.

Why it matters

BUDs under <797> come up any time a pharmacy compounds a sterile product for patient use. This includes hospital IV admixtures, chemotherapy preparations, total parenteral nutrition (TPN), intrathecal injections, and ophthalmic solutions. Administering a CSP past its BUD carries real risk: sterility can no longer be assured, and microbial contamination, degradation of active ingredients, or particulate formation may have occurred. Regulatory inspectors from state boards of pharmacy and accreditation bodies like The Joint Commission routinely audit BUD labeling compliance, and violations can result in citations or facility closure.

Example

A pharmacist compounds a single-dose vancomycin infusion bag in an ISO 5 cleanroom inside an ISO 7 buffer room. No sterility testing is performed, placing it in Category 1. Stored under refrigeration (2–8°C), USP <797> allows a maximum BUD of 4 days from the time of compounding. The label is printed with both the preparation date/time and a BUD four days out — any unused bags remaining after that point must be discarded.

Common confusions

BUD vs. expiration date: An expiration date is assigned by a manufacturer after stability testing on a sealed, commercially produced product. A BUD is assigned by the compounder and reflects sterility assurance limits, not just chemical stability — it can be much shorter than the ingredient expiration dates used to make the preparation.

USP <797> vs. USP <795>: <795> governs non-sterile compounding (creams, capsules, oral liquids) and uses the same "beyond-use date" terminology but applies entirely different criteria. The two chapters are not interchangeable.

BUD vs. in-use time: Some facilities add a separate "in-use" or "hang time" limit once a CSP is punctured or hung for infusion. This is distinct from the BUD, which governs the preparation before it is accessed.
