FIFO rotation — what it means for shelf life

What it means

FIFO rotation stands for "First In, First Out." It is a stock management practice where the oldest inventory — the items that arrived or were produced first — are used, sold, or consumed before newer stock. The goal is to ensure nothing sits unused so long that it expires or degrades before it can be put to use. In practical terms, this means placing new stock behind or beneath existing stock so that people naturally reach for the older items first.

Why it matters

FIFO rotation is critical anywhere perishable or time-sensitive products are stored. In a commercial kitchen, a grocery store cooler, a pharmacy, or a home pantry, failing to rotate stock properly leads to older items getting pushed to the back while newer arrivals get used up first. The older stock then sits until it spoils, expires, or loses quality — resulting in waste, potential health hazards, and financial loss. Regulatory bodies such as health departments and food safety agencies often require FIFO practices as part of standard food handling compliance. It's also important in manufacturing and warehousing, where components or raw materials can degrade over time.

Example

A restaurant receives a delivery of fresh dairy on Monday. They still have dairy from last Friday's delivery in the walk-in cooler. A cook practicing FIFO moves the Friday containers to the front of the shelf and places the Monday delivery behind them. When preparing meals throughout the week, staff reach for the front containers first — the Friday stock — ensuring it's used before it expires, while the Monday delivery waits its turn.

Common confusions

FIFO vs. LIFO: LIFO ("Last In, First Out") is the opposite approach — using the newest stock first. LIFO makes sense for some non-perishable inventory accounting situations but is almost never appropriate for physical perishable goods, since it guarantees older items expire unused.

FIFO rotation vs. FIFO accounting: In accounting, FIFO is a method for calculating the cost of goods sold, and it doesn't necessarily reflect how physical items are actually handled. A business can use FIFO accounting while practicing poor physical rotation, or vice versa. The two uses of the term are related in principle but operate independently.

Date labeling confusion: FIFO rotation depends on knowing which items arrived or were produced first. If products aren't clearly dated or labeled, staff can't reliably determine which stock is oldest, making proper rotation impossible even when the intent is there. This is why date-labeling systems are considered a prerequisite to effective FIFO practice.
