best by date — what it means for shelf life

What it means

A best by date is a manufacturer's estimate of when a product will be at its peak quality — flavor, texture, color, or potency — not a safety deadline. After this date, the product may lose some of its intended quality but is generally still safe to consume. It is a voluntary quality indicator, not a federally regulated expiration date (with the exception of infant formula in the U.S.).

Why it matters

Best by dates appear on a huge range of products: canned goods, snack foods, cereals, condiments, dairy, and more. Consumers and retailers use them to rotate stock and make purchasing decisions. Because many people mistake this label for a hard expiration cutoff, enormous amounts of perfectly safe food get thrown away each year. Understanding that "best by" is about quality, not safety, helps reduce unnecessary waste and unnecessary spending.

Example

A box of crackers stamped Best By: March 2025 was opened in May 2025. The crackers are slightly staler than when fresh but are not spoiled or unsafe to eat. Consuming them carries no health risk — they have simply passed their peak crunch window.

Common confusions

  • Best by vs. use by: A use by date is a stronger signal, often used on perishables like fresh meat or ready-to-eat deli items, and is closer to a safety recommendation. Best by is about quality; use by leans closer to safety. Still, even use by is not a universally regulated hard cutoff (except for infant formula).
  • Best by vs. sell by: A sell by date is instructions for the retailer, telling them how long to keep a product on the shelf. It says nothing direct about when you, the consumer, must use the item. A product bought on its sell-by date may still have days or weeks of good quality left.
  • Treating it as an expiration date: Many people discard food the moment it crosses a best by date. This is almost always unnecessary for shelf-stable or properly stored products. Use your senses — smell, appearance, and taste — as the real guide for whether food has genuinely gone bad.
