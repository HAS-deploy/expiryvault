Sunscreen, OTC meds, and first-aid kit expirations

Why Expiration Dates on Medicine Cabinet Staples Actually Matter

Most people know that prescription drugs expire, but the items sitting in beach bags, hiking packs, and bathroom cabinets — sunscreen, over-the-counter medications, and first-aid supplies — get far less scrutiny. They shouldn't. Some of them degrade in ways that are merely ineffective; others can become mildly harmful. Knowing which is which, and what the actual timelines are, lets you make a fast, informed call when you're standing there with a bottle in your hand.

Sunscreen Expiration

What the FDA says

The FDA requires sunscreen manufacturers to include an expiration date unless they've tested the product and can confirm it remains stable for at least three years. If there's no date printed on the bottle, you can treat that three-year window as your benchmark — but only if the sunscreen has been stored reasonably well (more on that below).

What actually degrades

Sunscreen works through either chemical UV filters (like avobenzone or oxybenzone) or physical/mineral filters (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide). Chemical filters are the more vulnerable ones. Avobenzone, for example, is notoriously unstable and begins breaking down with UV exposure and heat — sometimes faster than the labeled expiration if the product has been left in a hot car or baked in a beach bag all summer. Mineral filters are more stable but can still separate from the lotion base, making it impossible to apply them evenly.

The practical consequence: expired or heat-damaged sunscreen gives you a false sense of protection. You apply it, you think you're covered, and you burn.

Signs it's done regardless of the date

  • Smell has changed noticeably (chemical or rancid odor)
  • Texture is separated, gritty, or watery
  • Color has shifted significantly
  • It was stored in a hot car or direct sunlight for extended periods

The honest answer

If it's past the expiration date or shows any of the signs above, throw it out. Sunscreen is inexpensive compared to a bad burn or the long-term cost of cumulative UV damage.

Over-the-Counter Medications

The general principle

OTC drugs — pain relievers, antihistamines, antacids, cold medicines, sleep aids — are required by law to carry expiration dates. Those dates reflect the point through which the manufacturer guarantees full potency and safety under normal storage conditions (cool, dry, away from light). After that date, two things can happen: the drug loses potency, or in rare cases, it breaks down into compounds you don't want to take.

Pain relievers and fever reducers

Acetaminophen (Tylenol-type products): Generally considered one of the more stable OTC drugs. Studies of pharmaceutical stability suggest it retains most potency for a year or more past expiration if stored properly, though manufacturers won't vouch for it. The bigger risk here is dosing on something that's weaker than expected when you actually need it to work — say, for a high fever in a child.

Aspirin: This one has a well-known degradation pathway. Aspirin hydrolyzes over time into acetic acid and salicylic acid. The classic sign is a strong vinegar smell. If your aspirin smells like vinegar, it has broken down and should be discarded.

Ibuprofen: Similar to acetaminophen — relatively stable in solid tablet form, degrades faster in liquid suspension. Children's ibuprofen in liquid form expires more meaningfully than adult tablets. Pay attention to those dates.

Antihistamines

Diphenhydramine (the active ingredient in most OTC sleep aids and many allergy products) and loratadine/cetirizine are fairly stable in tablet form. They typically retain potency reasonably well past expiration, but again, there's no manufacturer guarantee. If you're relying on an antihistamine for a serious allergic reaction, this isn't the moment to roll the dice on an old pill.

Cold and cough preparations

Liquid formulations generally degrade faster than tablets and capsules. If you have a liquid cough syrup that's expired, discard it. The combination ingredients in multi-symptom products make degradation harder to predict.

What about storing medications in bathrooms?

Humidity and temperature fluctuations in bathrooms accelerate degradation. Manufacturers test under controlled conditions — typically 77°F and 60% relative humidity. Your bathroom after a hot shower is well outside those parameters. Store medications in a cool, dry place (a bedroom drawer or a dedicated cabinet outside the bathroom is genuinely better) and they'll come closer to hitting their labeled expiration.

Hard rule for specific OTCs

Discard these when expired without debate:

  • Liquid formulations of any kind
  • Products with measurable doses for children
  • Eye drops (sterility is a concern, not just potency)
  • Ear drops
  • Hydrogen peroxide (see below)

First-Aid Kit Supplies

Bandages and gauze

Dry, sealed bandages and gauze technically don't expire the way drugs do, but the adhesive on bandage strips degrades. Old bandages may not stick, which defeats the purpose. More importantly, if packaging is compromised — torn, damp, or discolored — sterility cannot be guaranteed. Check the seal, not just the date.

Antiseptic wipes and creams

Alcohol wipes: These evaporate over time, especially if the foil packaging has any micro-tears. An alcohol wipe that doesn't smell strongly of alcohol is probably not doing much. Check the packaging carefully.

Hydrogen peroxide: This one actually has an important expiration behavior. Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) breaks down into water and oxygen. An expired or improperly stored bottle may be little more than water. The test is simple: pour a small amount on a cut or a dark surface — it should bubble immediately. If it doesn't, it has degraded and won't do anything useful.

Antibiotic ointment: Bacitracin or combination antibiotic ointments have real expiration dates. The active ingredients can degrade, and the base can separate or become contaminated over time once opened. An opened tube that's more than a year old and has been sitting in a hot kit bag should go.

Elastic bandages (ACE wraps)

These don't have expiration dates typically, but the elastic degrades with age, washing, and repeated stretching. If the wrap has lost its recoil or feels brittle, it won't apply consistent compression.

Tweezers, scissors, and other tools

These don't expire, but they do rust and dull. A rusty splinter-removal tool introduces contamination. Check metal tools for rust, and replace anything that's corroded.

Instant cold packs

Most have a shelf life of 2–4 years. The chemical reaction (usually ammonium nitrate dissolving in water when the inner pouch breaks) can degrade. An old cold pack may not get cold enough to be useful. Check the date on anything you're planning to rely on in the field.

Building a Practical Inspection Routine

Rather than overhauling everything at once and then ignoring it for three years, a seasonal check takes about ten minutes and catches problems before they matter.

Twice a year (good moments: before summer, before cold/flu season): 1. Pull everything out and group it: sunscreen, OTC meds, first-aid supplies 2. Check expiration dates; flag anything expiring in the next six months 3. Check packaging integrity on bandages, wipes, and ointments 4. Smell the aspirin 5. Test the hydrogen peroxide 6. Check sunscreen texture and smell if it's been anywhere warm

After a trip or emergency use:

  • Restock anything you used
  • Replace anything that got wet, overheated, or had its packaging compromised

Disposing of Expired Products Properly

Don't flush medications down the toilet unless the label specifically says to (a small number of powerful drugs have that instruction). The preferred method is a drug take-back program — pharmacies and some police departments run these. If no option is available, the FDA recommends mixing medications with something undesirable (coffee grounds, dirt) in a sealed bag and discarding in household trash.

Sunscreen and first-aid supplies without drug components can generally go in regular trash.

FAQ

Can I use sunscreen that expired last year if it looks and smells fine? Probably not safely. Chemical filters degrade even without obvious signs. If it's a year past expiration, replace it — the cost of new sunscreen is trivial compared to the stakes.

Does ibuprofen go bad in a hot car? Heat accelerates degradation. A bottle that's spent the summer in a glove compartment has effectively aged much faster than one stored inside. If you can't confirm it was stored cool and dry, treat it as expired.

How long does an opened tube of antibiotic ointment last? Most manufacturers say one year after opening. Once opened, contamination risk increases. If you can't remember when you opened it, that's already a sign to replace it.

Are there any OTC medications that become dangerous after expiration, not just less effective? The well-known historical case involves a specific formulation of tetracycline that no longer exists, but for common OTC drugs the main risk is reduced effectiveness rather than toxicity. The exception is anything liquid or for children where dosing precision matters — don't gamble with those.

Is it worth buying a pre-assembled first-aid kit, or building my own? Pre-assembled kits are convenient but often contain supplies of uneven quality and no standardized expiration dates. Building your own lets you track dates from the start and stock what you actually use.
