Food expiration vs best-by vs use-by — what each actually means

The Labels on Your Food Are Not All Saying the Same Thing

Walk into any kitchen and you'll find a mix of dates stamped on packaging — some say "Best By," some say "Use By," some say "Sell By," and a few just say "Expires." Most people treat all of them as an expiration date and throw out food the moment the calendar flips past that number. That's a mistake that costs real money and wastes a lot of perfectly good food.

These labels mean fundamentally different things, and only one of them is genuinely about safety. Here's what each one actually means, how to read them correctly, and when you should actually be worried.

"Best By" — Quality, Not Safety

"Best By" (sometimes written as "Best Before") is a manufacturer's estimate of peak quality. It has nothing to do with whether the food is safe to eat.

The company is telling you: before this date, we're confident this product tastes exactly how we intended it to taste. After that date, the flavor, texture, or potency might gradually decline — but the food doesn't suddenly become dangerous.

Practical examples:

  • A bag of chips one week past its "Best By" date will likely taste slightly stale. It won't make you sick.
  • Canned beans two months past their "Best By" date are almost certainly fine. Canned goods can remain safe for years beyond the printed date as long as the can isn't bulging, rusted, or damaged.
  • Breakfast cereal, crackers, and dried pasta all fall into this category. They go stale; they don't go bad in a dangerous sense.

The rule of thumb: use your senses. Does it smell normal? Does it look normal? Taste a small amount. If everything checks out, it's fine to eat.

"Use By" — The One That Actually Matters for Safety

"Use By" is the label you should take seriously. This one is about safety, not just quality.

Manufacturers apply "Use By" dates to products that can harbor dangerous bacterial growth over time — particularly refrigerated, perishable items. The classic examples are:

  • Packaged deli meats
  • Refrigerated prepared meals
  • Fresh pasta in the refrigerator section
  • Baby formula (where it's strictly regulated)
  • Some dairy products

After the "Use By" date, bacteria like Listeria monocytogenes can reach levels that cause illness even if the food smells fine and looks fine. This is what makes "Use By" different from everything else: the food can be unsafe before it shows any obvious signs of spoilage.

Important nuance: If a product with a "Use By" date is frozen before that date, you extend its safe life significantly. The clock essentially pauses in the freezer. A package of ground beef with a "Use By" of tomorrow can go into the freezer today and be safe to use weeks later — just thaw it properly in the refrigerator.

Baby formula is a special case. The "Use By" date on formula is one of the few federally regulated food dates in the United States, and it exists because nutrient content can degrade, not just flavor. Follow it strictly.

"Sell By" — This Date Is for the Store, Not You

"Sell By" is a logistics and inventory instruction aimed at retailers, not consumers. The store is being told when to pull the product from the shelf to ensure the customer has reasonable shelf life remaining when they buy it.

When you see a "Sell By" date, there is typically still a meaningful amount of use time left in the product. General guidelines from food safety researchers:

| Product | Safe time after "Sell By" (refrigerated) | |---|---| | Milk | 5–7 days | | Eggs | 3–5 weeks | | Chicken | 1–2 days | | Ground beef | 1–2 days | | Hard cheese | 3–4 weeks |

The catch: this only holds if the food has been consistently properly stored. Milk that's been left on the counter for an hour every morning won't follow those numbers.

"Expires" or "Expiration Date" — Rare but Real

True expiration dates — where the product is genuinely considered unsafe or ineffective past a specific date — appear on a fairly short list of products:

  • Baby formula (again)
  • Over-the-counter medications and supplements
  • Prescription medications
  • Certain medical foods

For most grocery items, the word "expires" is used loosely by manufacturers to mean essentially what "Best By" means — a quality date with dramatic packaging language. Read it with that in mind.

Why the United States Has No Federal Standard (for Most Foods)

Here's what makes this genuinely confusing: in the United States, there is no federal law requiring date labels on most food products, and there's no standardized definition of what those labels must mean. Each manufacturer chooses their own language and their own methodology for setting the date.

This is slowly changing — there have been industry-wide pushes toward standardizing on just two labels ("Best If Used By" for quality and "Use By" for safety) — but plenty of older language remains in circulation. The USDA's Food Safety and Inspection Service oversees meat, poultry, and egg products, and even for those it doesn't mandate dates for most products.

The practical implication: when you're reading a date on packaged food, the label itself is your first clue to what the manufacturer actually means. "Use By" on deli meat means something real. "Best By" on a bag of pretzels is almost always just a quality marker.

How to Actually Decide Whether to Eat Something

Rather than relying on dates alone, use a layered approach:

1. Identify what type of food it is

High-risk perishables (raw meat, prepared deli foods, fresh dairy) require more caution than shelf-stable dry goods, canned foods, or frozen items.

2. Check what label is used and what it likely means

  • "Use By" on refrigerated protein — take it seriously.
  • "Best By" on dry or canned goods — much more flexible.

3. Use your senses

Smell the food. Look at it. Touch it if relevant. Most dangerous foodborne pathogens actually don't produce obvious off-smells (Listeria is the famous example), but obvious spoilage signs — sliminess on meat, visible mold, sour smell in milk — are still useful signals.

4. Consider how it was stored

Food stored at inconsistent temperatures, left out for long periods, or stored improperly degrades faster than dates assume. A "Use By" date is calculated assuming ideal refrigeration at or below 40°F (4°C) the entire time.

5. When in genuine doubt, throw it out

This applies especially to cooked leftovers (generally safe 3–4 days in the fridge), refrigerated proteins, and anything that shows any sign of abnormality.

Common Mistakes People Make

Throwing away shelf-stable food because of a "Best By" date. Canned tomatoes, dried beans, rice, pasta, and similar items are safe for months or years past these dates. The quality may decline but the food is not dangerous.

Ignoring "Use By" on refrigerated deli meats. These are genuinely risky after the date because of Listeria, which can grow even in cold refrigerator temperatures.

Assuming frozen food is safe forever. Freezing stops bacterial growth but doesn't stop freezer burn and quality degradation. Most frozen foods maintain best quality for 3–12 months.

Washing or cutting around mold on soft foods. Mold on soft foods (berries, soft cheese, bread, deli meats, cooked leftovers) typically means the entire item should go. Mold penetrates well beyond the visible surface in soft, moist foods. Hard cheeses and firm vegetables are different — you can cut an inch around visible mold and use the rest.

FAQ

Is it ever okay to eat meat past its "Use By" date? Not if it was kept refrigerated and the date has passed. If you froze it before the date, that's a different situation — the freeze effectively extends safe storage life.

Do eggs actually expire? Eggs have surprising longevity. A carton of eggs sold with a "Sell By" date is typically safe to eat 3–5 weeks after purchase if kept refrigerated. The float test (place in water — sinkers are fresh, floaters are old) is a rough freshness indicator but not a definitive safety test.

Can canned food really last for years? Yes. Properly stored, undamaged commercial canned foods maintain safety indefinitely, though quality declines over time. High-acid canned goods (tomatoes, fruit) typically stay at peak quality 12–18 months; low-acid goods (vegetables, meats) 2–5 years.

What about leftovers — is there a "date" rule? Cooked leftovers stored in the refrigerator at 40°F or below are generally safe for 3–4 days regardless of any labels. After that, the risk of bacterial growth increases meaningfully.

Why do different packages of the same product have different dates? Batch production — each production run gets its own date stamp based on when that batch was manufactured. Two identical cans produced two months apart will have dates two months apart.
